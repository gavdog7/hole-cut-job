Key Advantage — Hose-in-Hose Design

The Midea Duo uses a single hose-in-hose system: hot exhaust goes out through the inner channel while fresh intake air comes in through the outer channel. This means one hole, one hose — but it also means you cannot use a standard backdraft damper (it would block the intake). Plan for a screen-only vent or removable damper.

CRITICAL: The Hose Is Oval, Not Round — Sizing TBD

The Midea Duo's "hose-in-hose" system is NOT a simple round tube. It's an oval/oblong assembly with two channels side by side:

  • Inner round hose (~5" dia) — carries hot exhaust OUT
  • Outer intake channel (grille/louver beside the inner hose) — pulls fresh outside air IN

The back port of the unit measures approximately 9-10" wide × 7-8" tall. Both channels MUST reach outside for the unit to work as a dual-hose system. If you only route the 5" exhaust, you lose the fresh air intake and the Duo operates like an inferior single-hose unit.

We're using a two-phase approach: test with a 5" exhaust-only hole first, then either finish it or expand to 12" × 8" for full dual-hose. See the Walkthrough tab.

Critical: Cupboard Ventilation

The #1 mistake people make with closet AC installs: the unit needs to pull room air in and blow cooled air out. If the cupboard doors are closed, it'll just recirculate closet air and overheat. Plan to keep doors open, install louvered doors, or cut vents into the existing doors.

Understanding the Hose-in-Hose Assembly

The Midea Duo's hose-in-hose assembly is oval, measuring 12" wide × 8" tall at its widest when extended. It contains two channels side by side:

  • Inner round exhaust hose (~5" dia) — blows hot air OUT
  • Outer intake channel (grille section) — pulls fresh air IN from outside to cool the condenser

For Phase 1 (test), we route only the ~5" inner exhaust hose through a small round hole. The unit operates in single-hose mode — functional but less efficient.

For Phase 2B (full dual-hose), we expand the hole to 12" × 8" to route the entire oval assembly through the wall — both exhaust and intake reach outside for maximum efficiency.

Two-Phase Walkthrough

The Strategy: Test Before You Commit

Phase 1: Cut a simple 5" round hole for just the exhaust hose. No finishing, no vent cap — just get the unit running and evaluate cooling performance in single-hose mode.

Phase 2A (if cooling is fine): Finish the 5" hole properly — duct sleeve, vent cap, sealing. Done.

Phase 2B (if cooling isn't enough): Expand the hole to 12" × 8" to route the full oval hose-in-hose assembly through the wall for dual-hose operation.

A 5" round hole can always be expanded to a 12" × 8" rectangle later — but a 12" × 8" rectangle can't be shrunk. Test first.

PHASE 1 — Quick Test (Exhaust Only)

Goal: Get the unit running through the wall as fast as possible. Rough cut, no finishing. Just test whether exhaust-only cooling is good enough for your space.

1

Prep the Cupboard

  • Remove the HOTAS shelf and all gear — clear access to back wall
  • Position the Midea Duo on the floor of the cupboard
  • Measure the center of the inner round exhaust hose port on the back — that's your target height
  • Lay down a drop cloth for dust
2

Find Your Spot

  • Stud finder: Map the studs. Use wire detection mode — there's an outlet in this wall
  • Pick a stud bay: Center your hole in a bay (~14.5" clear). A 5" hole has tons of margin
  • Mark the height: Align with the center of the inner round exhaust port on the back of the unit
  • Important positioning: If you think you might expand later, offset the 5" hole to where the inner exhaust hose sits (left side of the oval port) — NOT dead center of the bay. This leaves room to expand rightward for the intake channel
Watch Out

Scan for wires AND plumbing. The outlet means electrical runs through this wall. Stay well clear — at least 1.5" from any detected wires.

3

Cut the 5" Test Hole

  • You can use the Milwaukee 6⅜" hole saw (gives a bit of clearance around the 5" hose) or even a smaller 5⅛" hole saw if you want a tighter fit
  • Drill from inside through the drywall — pilot bit punches through to mark the outside
  • Go outside, find the pilot hole, drill from outside through sheathing + siding
  • Clear insulation from the cavity
  • Don't bother with a duct sleeve yet — this is just a test
4

Route the Exhaust Hose

  • Detach the inner round exhaust hose from the oval assembly (if it separates) or just aim the exhaust end through the hole
  • Push the round hose end through the wall hole
  • It doesn't need to be pretty — just get the hot air path outside
  • Stuff some foam or a towel around the gap temporarily to reduce air leaks
  • The intake channel stays inside for now — the unit will pull room air across the condenser instead of outside air
5

Test It

  • Plug in and turn on the Midea Duo
  • Open the cupboard doors (or remove them) — the unit needs airflow
  • Let it run for at least 2-3 hours on a warm day
  • Note the room temperature over time — is it reaching your set point?
  • Go outside — verify hot exhaust is coming out the hole
  • Pay attention to whether the room feels stuffy or like doors are hard to open (signs of negative pressure from single-hose operation)
What You're Evaluating
  • Does the room cool to your target temp? If yes — single-hose may be fine for your space
  • Does it struggle on hot days? If it can't keep up when it's 90°F+ outside, the dual-hose upgrade will help
  • Do you notice negative pressure? Doors feeling sucked shut, hot air coming in from other rooms = single-hose limitation
  • Is the energy bill acceptable? The unit will run longer in single-hose mode — may matter over a full summer

Decision Point

After testing for a few days, is single-hose cooling good enough?

YES → Phase 2A
Finish the 5" hole properly
NO → Phase 2B
Expand to 12" × 8" for full dual-hose

PHASE 2A — Finish the 5" Round Hole

Cooling is good enough in single-hose mode. Now make it permanent and weatherproof.

6A

Install the Duct Sleeve

  • Remove the hose temporarily
  • Measure wall depth (drywall face to siding face)
  • Cut a 6" rigid metal duct pipe to that length with tin snips
  • Insert the sleeve through the hole
  • Seal interior side with expanding foam — fill the gap completely
  • Let foam cure, trim flush with a utility knife
7A

Mount Exterior Vent Cap

  • Apply clear silicone caulk around the duct opening on the exterior
  • Mount the 6" vent cap (InOvate Tan or Black recommended)
  • Secure with screws, caulk the flange edges
  • No backdraft damper — remove or pin it open so exhaust flows freely
8A

Seal Interior + Reconnect

  • Seal the hose-to-sleeve connection with HVAC foil tape
  • Reconnect the hose, keep it short and straight
  • Verify no leaks — feel around the connection with your hand while the unit runs
9A

Winter Seal

  • When AC season is over, disconnect the hose
  • Cut a Frost King magnetic vent cover to size
  • Stick it over the exterior vent cap — pops on/off in seconds

PHASE 2B — Expand to 12" × 8" (Full Dual-Hose)

Single-hose wasn't cutting it. Time to route the full oval hose-in-hose assembly through the wall.

Key Considerations for the Larger Opening
  • Stud bay width: 14.5" clear between studs — a 12" wide hole fits, but with only ~1.25" margin on each side. Measure carefully.
  • Tool change: The hole saw is no longer useful. Your oscillating multi-tool is the primary cutter for the rectangular opening.
  • Vent cap: You'll need a rectangular exterior vent cover (~12" × 8") instead of a round one. These exist for range hood venting.
  • The existing 5" hole becomes part of the larger opening — you're just expanding it, not starting over.
6B

Mark the Expanded Opening

  • From inside the cupboard, mark a 12½" × 8½" rectangle around the existing 5" hole (the extra ½" is clearance)
  • The existing hole should be positioned toward the left side of the rectangle (where the exhaust hose sits in the oval assembly)
  • Double-check with your stud finder — the rectangle must not hit a stud
  • Verify height alignment with the full oval port on the back of the unit
7B

Cut the Rectangular Opening

  • Use your oscillating multi-tool with a drywall blade
  • Cut along your marked rectangle from inside — the existing 5" hole gives you a starting point
  • Remove the drywall, clear insulation
  • Switch to a wood blade for the exterior sheathing
  • Cut from outside through sheathing + siding — go slowly on the visible siding
  • Clean up edges with the oscillating tool
8B

Frame and Sleeve the Opening

  • For a rectangular opening, you can't use a round duct sleeve
  • Option A: Line the opening with sheet metal flashing bent to fit — protects wood from moisture
  • Option B: Use a rectangular-to-round transition duct if you want to maintain a round exterior vent
  • Option C: Build a simple wood frame inside the opening and seal with polyurethane
  • Seal all gaps with expanding foam on the interior side
9B

Mount Rectangular Exterior Vent

  • You'll need a rectangular wall vent cover (~12" × 8" or 14" × 8") — these exist for range hood exhaust applications
  • Apply silicone caulk around the opening
  • Mount the vent cover with screws
  • Seal all edges with caulk
  • No backdraft damper — the intake channel needs airflow in both directions
10B

Connect the Full Hose Assembly

  • Route the complete 12" × 8" oval hose-in-hose assembly through the wall opening
  • Both channels (exhaust + intake) now reach outside
  • Seal around the hose with foam where it meets the interior wall
  • The unit now operates in full dual-hose mode — fresh outside air for condenser cooling, no room depressurization

BOTH PHASES — Cupboard Ventilation

!

Cupboard Airflow (Don't Skip!)

This applies regardless of which phase you end up with. The unit pulls room air in from the front grille and blows cooled air out the top. If the cupboard doors are closed, it recirculates a tiny volume and overheats.

  • Option A: Remove the doors entirely
  • Option B: Install louvered doors
  • Option C: Cut vent openings (top and bottom) into existing doors
  • Top vent lets cool air out; bottom vent lets room air in
